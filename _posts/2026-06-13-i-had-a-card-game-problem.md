And why I'm getting back into it

_Originally posted on Substack on 06/13/2026_

I’ve been lurking on Substack for a few months now and, despite joining with the hope of producing some kind of writing, I haven’t felt that spark of inspiration I imagine all the people I follow on here must feel before they start typing. I always thought a real writer, whatever that is, should be able to output interesting words on any subject they’re presented with. After all, that’s what all my essay-based tests were like in high school. This assumption could be true, but even if it is indeed a skill that belongs to an expert writer, a creeping realization has been occupying my thoughts, especially during the long pauses in which I try to write down non-trivial comments about the latest book I’ve read on Goodreads; it might only be acquired after hundreds of hours dedicated to the craft. And I guess this realization must have been the flint stone that produced that long-awaited spark.

In the end, instead of picking a random topic among the many in vogue, which was my initial idea, I’ve decided to write about something personal. Actually, now that I think about it, individual experiences seem to be the topic in vogue on Substack, so I didn’t have all that many choices to begin with.

---

I’m currently pursuing a master’s degree in computer science. The course takes place in a fairly big university campus, by Italian standards, in a fairly small city, also by Italian standards. It’s the same campus where I obtained my undergraduate degree in the same field, so I’ve been there for a few years, and during those years, the activity that was most likely to keep me away from my studies was a card game known locally as _Maraffa_. I can recall a few days when I could only study without interruption for 30 minutes before feeling a light tap on my shoulder and seeing someone, most likely a different person each time, holding a deck and pointing towards it with an inviting smile. Maraffa is not its only name; it seems to change every 50 kilometers or so. In my hometown, it goes by _Beccacino_, and if you keep traveling north, you’ll hear names like _Trionfo_ and _Marafone_.

![A deck of playing cards from Emilia-Romagna]({{ site.url }}/assets/images/2026-06-13/deck.webp)

Maraffa, or whatever you prefer to call it, is a 4-player game played with the traditional Italian playing cards, the ones that resemble a tarot deck and are 40 in total. Players are paired into two teams, and players on the same team must be seated opposite each other. The first team to reach 31 or 41 points, depending on how long you want the game to be, wins the match. At the start of every hand, each player has 10 cards. The first player of the first hand is always the one holding the four of coins, then that role is shifted counter-clockwise at every hand. The first player of each hand also gets to pick the _briscola_ suit for said hand, a rule I won’t explain in detail for the sake of brevity, just know that the first played card of each turn sets the commanding suit, and the team that plays the highest card of said suit gets to keep all the points played that turn. All players must play a card belonging to the commanding suit, unless they are not holding any, in which case we say that player is _flying_. A flying player can play any suit, but his card won’t count towards winning the turn, unless it belongs to the briscola suit, which overrides all others.

The three is the highest card, followed by the two and the ace, then we have the king, the knight, and the knave, all of which are valued at one third of a point, except for the ace, which is a full point. All other cards follow their number in power (the six of cups is higher than the five of cups, etc.) and score zero points. The team that wins the last round gets an extra point, for a total of eleven and two-thirds. If all of these rules were extremely confusing to you, do not go over them again; there are a few more I haven’t included, but you could ignore them all and still get the point of my little writing exercise. However, one rule that is fundamentally important for what I’m trying to say here is that players can’t talk to each other, except for a few specific words that can be replaced by hand gestures, making Maraffa a completely taciturn game.

I hope you get the picture by now. Imagine four men seated at a little square table in a seaside bar. Retired, all of them, and unhealthily tan. They’re clearly playing a card game, but no one says a word. Someone swears loudly now and then, or slams the table in frustration, and yet, other than that, not a peep. Then, the last card of the current hand is played, and the men’s tongues are untied. The table becomes the meeting place to debate a decades-long argument. The voices stack on top of each other, droplets of spit hit the surface of the table, and quickly evaporate in the hard-hitting sun. This screenplay is being rehearsed almost every hour of every weekday on campus by a myriad of students.

![Masters of the art]({{ site.url }}/assets/images/2026-06-13/players.webp)

Obviously, it’s not the only card game the students like to play here. I’ve even seen a few poker games recently. But, it is by far the most common. I like to believe every student has been asked whether they know the rules to it by a triplet looking for a fourth player. This is a practice canonically known as “_looking for the fourth_”. Some say the corridors are haunted by the phantoms of students who have died before graduating because they refused to start their afternoon studies before playing a match, but couldn’t find a fourth. Pale apparitions floating through the classroom walls, holding a deck.

I’m not sure how the trend started, and at this point, it’s more of a tradition than a trend. I do, however, have a wordy opinion on why the game is so fun, not just to play, but also to watch. Firstly, as any game should be, it is easy to learn, but hard to master. The paragraph above about the rules might have scared you, and the Italian-ness of it all could be a bit off-putting to those of you concerned with the possibility of cultural appropriation, but really, if you own a deck and can count to ten, you can play. No knowledge of the Italian language required (you can’t talk, remember?). Becoming an above-average player, however, probably takes many hours of play. I would estimate that it has taken me a year to go from an absolute beginner to the skill level of the average player on campus.

---

**Here’s a quick strategy guide for beginners, by me:**

The first step to earning your black belt is to start counting cards. This isn’t an old gambler’s dirty trick; it’s the only viable strategy: your teammate can’t tell you what cards they have, so you must infer them. Start small, only count the cards of the briscola suit; if you can remember how many have been played already, add the amount you have in your hand, and subtract the result from ten. Now you know how many of them are still in play. This is most useful to the player who picks the briscola. Most players choose the suit that they hold the most of. Four is ok, five or more is really good, and three is the worst-case scenario (and no, the highest number suit in your hand cannot be less than three because you start with ten cards and the deck has four suits; you’re gonna have to crunch numbers in your head a lot faster than that if you wanna win, kid). If it’s your turn to pick the briscola suit, you most likely want to get to the point where you’re the only player holding any briscola cards at all, which guarantees a win on any turn they’re played in. Forcing the other players to play their briscola cards is known as smontare; if your teammate is any good, they’ll know you want to do this and help you out, and this is where the allowed codewords/hand gestures come into play. Once you’ve developed that little buffer in your brain that can hold the briscola suit, make one more for each of the other three suits, and you’ll be counting all the cards in the deck before graduation day. The next step is a little harder, but it’s fairly similar. You have to remember exactly what cards have been played for each suit; if you can’t do that, at least remember what the highest card left is for every suit. By playing the highest card in a suit, the round is yours, unless the enemy team plays a briscola card, which is called cutting, by the way, and the highest card is a good card. And even if your team doesn’t have a good card, if you know your teammate is flying in a certain suit, you can play it to let him cut it. I know this seems like an insane amount of things to keep in mind, and at first, it is. But you’ll learn to do these things gradually. After you reach this point, your doom-scrolling, atrophied grey matter will be trained to the point of being able to grow new brain cells, and you can finally start the final phase, the fifth Dan of Maraffa: remembering what cards each player has played and in what turn. This information allows you to correctly guess the majority of the hands of all other players.

Maraffa is not all about skill; it’s a card game after all, and luck plays a big role. But it’s still possible to make the best out of a losing hand, and blunder a winning one. With practice comes the chance to even out the bell curve of distribution. After a few hundred games, you stop thinking of individual cards, but their combinations and patterns of such combinations. Once a pattern is engraved in your memory, it becomes a zipped folder, its size being smaller than the size of the cards that compose it. The pattern replaces the card as your unit of measurement.

Another way of learning is to watch people play. Find a table where a match is taking place, pick a player, and hover behind them. You’ll notice everybody plays a little differently. Each time I spectate, I see someone choose a card I would consider blasphemous. I tell them so, and they proceed to explain their reasoning. Sometimes, I learn something new; other times they learn from me. But what makes observing a Maraffa game truly interesting is catching on to people’s mannerisms. The first thing you notice is the way they hold their cards. We all group them by suit, but the similarity ends there. I hold mine by ascending numerical order from left to right. I get a lot of comments about this, mostly negative, since most people prefer to sort theirs in order of power.

By paying a little more attention, you notice how people play their cards. Some toss them, others let them slide across the table, or accompany them all the way to its center. They tend to behave differently based on the card being played. There’s usually more emotion portrayed through a winning card’s toss, like excitement and joy. With a bad card comes frustration or disappointment. After associating how these sentiments travel from each player to their cards, you can make an educated guess on what card they're about to play. You’ll be surprised by the number of correct guesses.

---

I look back on the years of my bachelor’s degree with some resentment. It took me two years longer than it could have, and that fact makes me feel inadequate. I know they don’t think about it that way, but sometimes I wonder if my family feels the same. I try to find reasons for that delay, and all I can find are excuses. One of which is that addictive card game. It’s obviously not the reason; it might be an accomplice, but it certainly isn’t the mastermind. It probably had more of a positive impact if you consider all the new acquaintances I’ve met through the game. Thanks to the no speaking rule, having a conversation while playing Maraffa forces you to talk about anything that isn’t the game. It’s basically a friendship ritual; I can’t think of any other game, cards or no cards, that not only allows that, but encourages it.

I’ve been cutting down on the number of matches I participate in lately; some days I don’t play at all. My grades have gone up since the start of my master’s, but I don’t believe that’s a result of my card game diet. Yes, the number of hours of daily study has gone up, but I still have a bunch of free time left where I could fit in almost the same number of matches as before. I’m thinking a few more hands now and then couldn’t hurt.
